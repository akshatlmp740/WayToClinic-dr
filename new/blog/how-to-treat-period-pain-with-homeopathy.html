What is Period Pain?

Menstrual cramps or dysmenorrhea is defined as the pain that women feel during their menstrual period. This pain is usually localized and characterised by throbbing or cramping pain in the lower abdomen and is usually mild to moderate.

It begins a day or two before the first day of menstruation or may extend for several days. This pain is often localized in the lower abdomen, although women can also have other symptoms, including lower back pain, nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, and headache.

There are two types: primary, which is normal and does not indicate an underlying problem, and secondary, caused by other conditions such as endometriosis or fibroids. Dysmenorrhea and mild pain during the menstrual cycle are quite common, but severe and disabling pain should be reported to a doctor.

Symptoms of Period Pain

  • Lower Back Pain: Pain that spreads out towards the lower back.
  • Thigh Pain: Painful contractions, which may be of pinpoint like character or more like deep tender sore feeling in the inner thigh.
  • Nausea and Vomiting: Others may experience nauseous feelings or, worse still, they may even have to vomit.
  • Diarrhea or Loose Stools: Bowel habits can be altered at times, usually because of hormonal fluctuations.
  • Headaches: Most women report experiencing tension headaches or migraines during their period.
  • Fatigue: Frequent colds and general fatigue or low energy levels are normal.
  • Mood Changes: This is one of the irregular, unpredictable cognitive features of confusing accessibility; it is possible to be alternately irritable or even sad in addition to having physical symptoms.

Causes of Period Pain

Period pain can be categorized into two main types. In women, two types of dysmenorrhea, primary and secondary dysmenorrhea, originate from different backgrounds.

Primary dysmenorrhea is a common condition characterized by menstrual pain that generally does not have associated comorbidities. It arises from an overproduction of prostaglandins, which are hormone-like substances released by the uterus.

Key points include:

  • Prostaglandins: High levels lead to increased uterine contractions.
  • Pain Characteristics: This results in pain and weak contractions felt during menstruation.
  • Improvement Over Time: The pain often improves with age or after childbirth in some cases.
  • Timing of Relief: Pain usually subsides shortly after menstruation begins.

Secondary dysmenorrhea, on the other hand, is due to contractual diseases to the reproductive system of the woman. Common causes include:

  • Endometriosis: Pathological state in which endometrial-like tissue develops at sites other than the uterus and causes much pain.
  • Uterine Fibroids: Benign tumors of the uterus that can lead to some discomfort during menstruation and pain.
  • Adenomyosis: A complication of pregnancy in which the endometrium extends into the myometrium and causes discomfort and persistent bleeding.
  • Pelvic Inflammatory Disease (PID): One of the diseases that afflict some people in the reproductive system leading to painful cramps.
  • Cervical Stenosis: A constriction of the cervical canal that causes difficulties in the shedding of endometrium and raises intrauterine pressure.

Secondary dysmenorrhea begins at a later age and may become more severe with time. There are situations where period pain becomes unbearable, or in case there is a change in pattern, it would be fitting to seek the help of a doctor.
