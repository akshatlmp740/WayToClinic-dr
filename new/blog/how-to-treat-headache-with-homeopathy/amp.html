What is a Headache?

Headache is a painful and uncomfortable sensation arising from the head, scalp, or neck. Though mild and severe tinnitus, it may affect one side of the head or both and is a common disease. This is because headaches can be caused by stress, dehydration, lack of sleep, or even medical conditions.

Headaches are classified as tension headaches, migraine, that is characterized by throbbing pain and nausea, and cluster headaches, which are characterized by severe pain around one eye. There is also a primary headache which can be easily cured or may need some medication.

Symptoms of Headache

Here are the common symptoms associated with different types of headaches:

  • Tension Headaches:
    • Pain tends to be mild to moderate, and patients usually complain of a throbbing, low-grade discomfort.
    • Headache is normally on both sides of the head.
    • Pressing or constricting feeling around the forehead or back of the head and neck.
    • Normally, it does not produce vomiting or photophobia.
  • Migraine Headaches:
    • Severe, pounding headache, generally localized on the side of the head.
    • Duration is variable and can be 4 hours, 2 days, or 3 days.
    • Accompanied by symptoms such as nausea or vomiting.
  • Cluster Headaches:
    • Soreness, stinging, or stabbing, usually experienced in an area around one eye.
    • These are characterized by cycles in which several headaches occur in a single day.
    • Symptoms may include red and watery eyes.
  • Sinus Headaches:
    • Chronic or severe diffuse pain in the cheekbones, forehead, or bridge of the nose.
    • Pain generally aggravates on forward bending or on lying down.
    • Especially when coupled with other signs of a sinus infection, such as nasal discharge, fever, and facial puffiness.
  • Chronic Daily Headaches:
    • Happens 15 days and over per month for more than three months.
    • These may be chronic migraines, chronic tension headaches, and new daily persistent headaches.
  • Post-Traumatic Headaches:
    • Appear after a head injury, and, depending on the severity and type of the injury, it can be mildsevere and temporaryprolonged.
    • Some may mimic tension-type headaches, migraines, or any other type of headache that is experienced.

Causes of Headache

Here are the primary causes of headaches:

  • Stress: Stress can cause emotional stress, leading to tight muscles that tend to hurt more easily or even get headaches.
  • Muscular Tension: Sitting in a single position for several hours, slouching, lack of exercise, or even holding a bad posture will cause the muscles on the neck and shoulders to tense, leading to tension headaches.
  • Dietary Factors: Food and beverages that may cause headaches are those with alcohol, specifically red wine, aged cheese, processed meats, and foods containing MSG. Some other factors could be a lack of diets, lack of meals, and differences in blood sugar levels also play a role.
  • Dehydration: Another possible reason for headaches is poor water intake: dehydration is a real problem in many respects, and one of them is headaches.
  • Hormonal Changes: Hormonal imbalance in women, for instance, during the menstruation period or menopause, can lead to migraines and other kinds of headaches.
  • Sleep Disturbances: If a patient does not get enough sleep or does not get enough restful sleep, the chances of getting a headache will increase.
  • Environmental Factors: People with headaches are also sensitive to different stimuli, such as light, noise, certain smells, and climatic changes.
  • Medical Conditions: Secondary headaches can arise from such illnesses as sinus problems, high blood pressure, sicknesses such as meningitis, or down to neurological disorders such as tumours.
  • Medications: Some drugs should help reduce headaches, but if the effects wear off or the patient stops taking the drugs, the headache comes back with a vengeance.
  • Eye Strain: Eye strain is a common result of extended hours of watching television or computers or un-prescribed vision affliction, and this causes headaches.
  • Caffeine Withdrawal: Caffeine consumed on a daily basis, for example, in coffee and tea, may cause headaches when it is withdrawn suddenly.
  • Infections: Other headaches arise from illnesses, for instance, sinusitis or the flu, that cause inflammation, which places pressure on the head.
  • Trauma: Injury to any part of the head, neck, or spine causes headaches due to inflammation of sensitive areas.
  • Other Triggers: Other triggers of the headache include smoking, alcohol hangovers, and swinging between temperatures can also cause headache episodes.
