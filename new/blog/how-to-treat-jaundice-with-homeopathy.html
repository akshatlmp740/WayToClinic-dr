How To Treat Jaundice With Homeopathy?

By Dheeraj Sharma July 9, 2024 Medical Conditions

What is Jaundice?

Jaundice is a condition where the skin and eyes develop a yellowish hue due to high levels of bilirubin in the body. Bilirubin, a yellow compound, is produced when red blood cells break down. Normally, the liver processes bilirubin and expels it through bile in the stools. \

However, if the liver is inflamed or the bile ducts are blocked, bilirubin accumulates, leading to jaundice. Common symptoms include yellowing of the skin and eyes, dark urine, and itching. Since jaundice can indicate serious issues like liver disease, seeking medical attention is crucial.

How Do You Know If You Have Jaundice?

Recognizing jaundice involves noticing changes in your skin and eyes. These changes are due to varying levels of bilirubin in your blood. Moderate levels cause a yellow tint, while very high levels result in a brownish hue. You may also notice dark urine and pale stools. Additionally, jaundice often comes with other symptoms that can help identify it;

  • You may feel unusually tired and need more energy.
  • There can be discomfort or pain in the upper abdomen, often due to liver inflammation.
  • Unexpected weight loss can occur
  • Feeling nauseous and vomiting is common
  • You might experience a high-temperature
  • Itching can be so intense that it affects sleep or causes skin damage from scratching.

What Are The Types Of Jaundice?

Jaundice can be classified into three main types. Each type has distinct causes and characteristics.

Pre-Hepatic Jaundice

Pre-hepatic jaundice occurs when red blood cells are excessively broken down. This overproduction of bilirubin overwhelms the liver’s ability to conjugate it. While the conjugated bilirubin is excreted normally, the unconjugated bilirubin builds up in the bloodstream, leading to jaundice.

Hepatocellular Jaundice

Hepatocellular jaundice results from the liver’s inability to properly process bilirubin. This can happen when liver cells are damaged or dysfunctional. In severe cases, the liver may become cirrhotic, causing compression of the intrahepatic bile ducts and further obstructing bile flow. This leads to a mix of conjugated and unconjugated bilirubin in the blood.

Post-Hepatic Jaundice

Post-hepatic jaundice is caused by an obstruction in the bile ducts, preventing the normal drainage of bile from the liver. The secreted bilirubin is conjugated, but it cannot be excreted properly. This results in high levels of conjugated bilirubin in the blood, leading to jaundice.

Symptoms Of Jaundice

Jaundice manifests through several noticeable symptoms that can help in its identification. These symptoms vary in severity and can indicate the underlying cause and extent of the condition;

  • Your skin and the whites of your eyes may develop a yellowish hue, which can range from mild to intense.
  • In more severe cases, the whites of your eyes might take on an orange or brown color.
  • Your urine may become significantly darker than usual
  • Stools may appear lighter in color, often described as clay-colored
  • You may experience extreme tiredness and lack of energy
  • In severe cases, jaundice can cause nausea and vomiting

What Causes Jaundice?

Jaundice occurs when bilirubin builds up in the blood, leading to yellowing of the skin and eyes. This condition can arise from various underlying issues, each affecting the body in different ways.

Causes of Jaundice in Newborns

In newborns, a less developed liver may not effectively filter out excess bilirubin. While symptoms often subside within two weeks without treatment, severe cases may require phototherapy or blood transfusions to prevent kernicterus, a rare type of permanent brain damage.

Causes of Jaundice in Adults

  • Hepatitis: Hepatitis, caused by viral infections, autoimmune disorders, or drugs, can be either acute or chronic. Over time, this inflammation can damage the liver, resulting in jaundice.
  • Alcohol-Related Liver Disease: Excessive alcohol consumption can damage the liver, leading to conditions like alcoholic cirrhosis and alcoholic hepatitis, both of which can cause jaundice.
  • Blocked Bile Ducts: Bile ducts, which carry bile from the liver and gallbladder to the small intestine, can become blocked by gallstones, cancer, or rare liver diseases, leading to jaundice.
  • Pancreatic Cancer: Pancreatic cancer, a common cancer in people, can block the bile ducts, causing jaundice.
  • Certain Medicines: Medications like penicillin, acetaminophen, steroids, and birth control pills can result in liver damage or jaundice.

Rare Health Conditions Causing Jaundice

  • Dubin-Johnson Syndrome: This chronic condition prevents the excretion of conjugated bilirubin from the liver.
  • Pseudojaundice: A benign form of jaundice, pseudojaundice is caused by consuming large amounts of carotene-rich foods like carrots, pumpkins, or melons.
  • Crigler-Najjar Syndrome: This inherited condition impairs the body’s ability to process bilirubin, leading to jaundice.

Diet and Lifestyle Changes to Deal with Jaundice

Managing jaundice requires careful attention to both diet and lifestyle. These changes help support liver function and promote recovery. Below are some dietary and lifestyle adjustments to consider.

Proper nutrition is crucial in helping your liver recover from jaundice. Implementing the following dietary changes can aid in your recovery;

  • Drink plenty of water and fluids to stay well-hydrated. Proper hydration helps the liver flush out toxins.
  • Consume easily digestible foods like carbohydrates and fruits such as mango and papaya. These foods do not strain the liver.
  • During jaundice, it’s important to avoid fatty and oil-rich foods, which can burden the liver.
  • Include high-fiber foods, as they help move bile out of the liver, reducing toxicity. These include cruciferous vegetables like kale and broccoli, berries, oatmeal, almonds, brown rice, and quinoa.
  • Moderate consumption of coffee or herbal tea can improve liver health by reducing the risk of cirrhosis and lowering harmful liver enzyme levels.
  • Eat at least 2 ½ cups of vegetables and 2 cups of fruits daily. Grapefruit, avocado, Brussels sprouts, grapes, and mustard greens are healthy choices for jaundice.
  • Avoid refined carbohydrates and sugary foods like pasta, soda, white bread, and baked goods, as they can strain the liver.
  • Choose lean proteins instead of fatty meats, which are harder to digest and can negatively impact liver health.

Lifestyle Changes to Manage Jaundice

Adopting healthy lifestyle habits is essential in supporting liver health and managing jaundice. Here are some key lifestyle changes:

  • Alcohol is harmful to the liver and should be completely avoided during jaundice.
  • Practice good hygiene to prevent infections that can worsen jaundice. Avoid potentially contaminated water and food.
  • Getting vaccinated for hepatitis can help prevent liver infections that lead to jaundice.
