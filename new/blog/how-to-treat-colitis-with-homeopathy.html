What is Colitis?

Colitis is simply a disease whereby the colon or the large intestine experiences inflammation. This inflammation can cause a number of symptoms, such as tummy pains, diarrhea, and sometimes even blood in your stools.

This disease can be attributed to microbial pathogens, and other diseases include inflammatory bowel diseases like ulcerative colitis and Crohn’s disease. Other causes may be such as circulation of blood to the colon being restricted or side effects from drugs. The intensity of symptoms ranges from slight to severe and refers to bloating, fever, and other signs that tell the patient he needs a bowel movement.

The treatment is usually based on the root cause and may, therefore, require the administration of anti-inflammatories or antibiotics, among others. Left untreated, colitis may produce dangerous complications, so it is advisable to consult your healthcare provider if you have the indications.

Symptoms of Colitis

Colitis, or inflammation of the colon, manifests in symptoms that vary by cause. These include:

  • Diarrhea: Often, with bloody or mucous.
  • Abdominal Pain: Hernia or pain or discomfort in the lower abdomen.
  • Rectal Bleeding: Blood in stool is common.
  • Bowel Urgency: The urge to pass stool suddenly and forcefully with nearly irresistible pressure to do so.
  • Weight Loss: Because of the appetite lack of nutrients’ assimilation.
  • Fatigue: It results from a lack of water or lack of nutrients in the body.

Causes of Colitis

Colitis, or colon inflammation, can result from several factors:

  • Infections: Bacteria in contaminated food or water such as C. difficile, Salmonella, or E. coli.
  • Inflammatory Bowel Diseases (IBD): Chronic Illnesses such as ulcerative colitis and Crohn’s disease.
  • Ischemic Colitis: Some examples of obstructing blood flow are blockages or clots of the blood vessels.
  • Radiation Therapy: Medical application to give radiation to cancer patients.
  • Food Allergies/Sensitivities: Reactions to specific foods.
  • Antibiotics: Infection with pathogenic flora, for example, Clostridium difficile.
  • Stress & Diet: Lack of proper diet and stress are known to cause the condition to worsen.
  • Genetics: IBD in the family contributes to the issue.
