Hydrastis Canadensis – Uses, Dosage, Side Effects, Composition, And More

By Deeksha Katiyar September 1, 2024 Medicine

What is Hydrastis Canadensis?

Hydrastis canadensis is also known as Goldenseal, orangeroot, or yellow puccoon. It is a type of perennial herb from the buttercup family known as Ranunculaceae, which is native to North America. The Hydrastis canadensis plant is distinguished by its thick, yellow-knotted rootstock. The stem of Hydrastis canadensis is purplish and hairy above ground and yellow below ground, where it connects to the yellow rhizome.

Goldenseal or Hydrastis canadensis reproduces both clonally through the rhizome and sexually, with clonal division more frequent than asexual reproduction. It has a fine hairy, upright, unbranched stem structure that grows to 15–50 centimeters (6–19+1⁄2 inches) in length. A normal fertile plant of Hydrastis canadensis has a single stem with two palmately lobed leaves.

Hydrastis Canadensis Chemical Constituents

The major chemical constituents of Hydrastis canadensis are the alkaloids berberine and hydrastine, hydrastinine, and canadine. Extracts of hydrastis canadensis contain approximately equal concentrations of about 17 mM of two methylenedioxyphenyl alkaloids, berberine, and hydrastine, inhibited with increasing potency the isoform CYP2C9 (diclofenac 4β-hydroxylation) CYP2D6 (bufuralol 1β-hydroxylation) and CYP3A4 (testosterone 6ß-hydroxylation) activities in human hepatic microsomes (Chatterjee and Franklin, 2003), with interpolated IC50 values of 0.98%, 0.66%, and 0.18%, respectively.

Hydrastis Canadensis Preparation

Hydrastis canadensis is prepared through multiple modes, and the method of preparation depends on the treatment being done by the use of it.

  • Tincture: Hydrastis canadensis is finely chopped, placed in a glass jar, and filled with alcohol. Now seal the jar and keep it in a dark place for about 4-6 weeks, then strain it and consume it.
  • Tea or Infusion: Add 1-2 teaspoons of dried goldenseal root per cup of water in boiled water. Let it simmer in water, and then strain the mixture.
  • Powder: Grind the dried goldenseal root into a fine powder and then store it in an airtight container away from moisture and light.

Hydrastis Canadensis Dosage

Hydrastis canadensis is found in many forms, such as capsules, lotions, drops, sprays, and eyewash. People intake hydrastis canadensis in different quantities suitable to them, but there isn’t much research to determine the optimal dosage.

  • Generally, the dried root supplements of hydrastis canadensis are taken in doses ranging from 0.5 to 10 grams about three times a day.
  • The alcoholic tinctures and liquid extracts of hydrastis canadensis are usually consumed in doses of 0.3 to 10 mL about three times daily.
  • Goldenseal or Hydrastis canadensis can also be made into tea by mixing and boiling 2 teaspoons of the dried herb of Hydrastis canadensis in 1 cup (240 mL) of hot water for about 15 minutes.

The right dosage of hydrastis canadensis for children varies depending on the child’s age, weight, and the form of the herb being used (e.g., tincture, powder, or capsule). A general adult dose might be around 500 mg to 1,000 mg of the dried root of hydrastis canadensis per day, so the child’s dose would be adjusted accordingly. However, exact dosages should be determined by a healthcare provider to ensure its safety.

Who Should Avoid Hydrastis Canadensis?

Hydrastis canadensis, is a widely used medicinal herb that has been used for various purposes, including as a dietary supplement. As Hydrastis canadensis offers various potential health benefits, it is still important to be aware of the individuals who should avoid using this:

  • Pregnant and breastfeeding women: The uterine-stimulating effects of hydrastis canadensis can potentially cross the placenta, posing a risk to the developing fetus. Hence, it is suggested to avoid the use of hydrastis canadensis for pregnant women. Also, it may be secreted in breast milk and affect the nursing infant, so it is advised not to use it.
  • Children and infants: Due to a lack of research on the functioning of the Hydrastis canadensis, it is recommended not to be taken for children.
  • Individuals with certain medical conditions: People who have diabetes should take caution and consult with their healthcare provider before using it, as it may increase blood sugar levels, causing further complications. It also has an anticoagulant effect, which increases the risk of bleeding. Individuals with bleeding disorders or those taking blood-thinning medications should avoid using it. The hepatotoxic (liver-damaging) effects of hydrastis canadensis might cause some problems for people with existing liver conditions.
  • Individuals with sensitivity or allergies: If some people already have some known allergy or sensitivity to hydrastis canadensis, it should be avoided.

Side Effects And Risks Of Using Hydrastis Canadensis

  • Various gastrointestinal side effects, such as nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, and stomach discomfort, might be observed after the overdose consumption of Hydrastis canadensis.
  • The consumption of Hydrastis canadensis might affect blood pressure and heart rate, so people already who have some history of cardiovascular diseases should avoid using it.
  • The neurological effects, such as headaches, dizziness, and fatigue, might be caused particularly by prolonged use or in high doses of Hydrastis canadensis.
  • Experiencing skin irritation, rashes, or photosensitivity (increased sensitivity to sunlight) with the use of hydrastis canadensis might be one of its complications.
  • Hydrastis canadensis may also interact with a variety of medications, including prescription drugs, over-the-counter medications, and other herbal supplements.
