What is HMPV Virus?

HMPV stands for Human Metapneumovirus and is a type of respiratory virus that mainly infects the lungs, though it can impact any age. First identified in 2001, it is transmitted by respiratory droplets through coughs or sneezes and contact with infected surfaces. Like many viruses, the symptoms of HMPV are much like having a severe cold and can include coughing, fever, sore throat, and Shortness of breath.

For most patients with the above diseases, the infection is mild and clears up without any specific treatments, but the following patients are at a higher risk for severe illness: HMPV symptoms are frequent in winter and early spring, and the infection can cause severe diseases such as pneumonia in high-risk groups.

Symptoms of HMPV Virus

Symptoms of Human Metapneumovirus (HMPV) infection typically include:

  • Cough : Sometimes present as chronic or recurrent, at other times associated with mucus production.
  • Fever: Usually mild to moderate.
  • Runny or Stuffy Nose: Nasal congestion is common.
  • Sore Throat : This may lead to some level of discomfort and or inflammation.
  • Wheezing: This may be experienced, particularly by patients with more serious conditions.
  • Shortness of Breath: One can have a problem with breathing, which is, as a result, preferred to the vulnerable docket.
  • Fatigue: Someone suffering from cholera may complain of general weakness and muscle achings.

Like most advanced human parainfluenza viruses, HMPV can cause bronchiolitis or pneumonia if the situation gets worse [especially among children, older people, and those with low immunity. Signs usually develop 3- 6 days after contact and usually range from 3-28 days in duration and only require supportive treatment.

Causes of HMPV Virus

Human Metapneumovirus (HMPV) is known to be contracted directly from infected people through respiratory secretion expelled through air by coughing or sneezing. It is also possible to get infected by touching an object contaminated surfaces like doorknobs, countertops, toys, etc, with respiratory secretions from the affected person.

HMPV is seasonal, with outbreaks peaking during the winter and early spring, which is also the time of the year when RSV and influenza are most active.

HMPV can affect anyone, but some people are at a greater risk of severe illness, particularly children under the age of five, older people, and those with compromised immune systems. The incubation period for HMPV ranges from 3 to 6 days and the patient may be potentially infective in the absence of clinical manifestations.

How Common is HMPV Virus?

It is believed that HMPV is responsible for 10%-12% of respiratory tract infections in children. Several complications are likely, and while the overwhelming majority of cases are mild, 5-16% of children are likely to suffer respiratory problems, and those may include lower respiratory infections such as pneumonia. Such severe forms are most commonly observed in children, older people, and patients with immunodeficiency.
