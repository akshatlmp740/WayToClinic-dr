Foeniculum Vulgare – Uses, Dosage, Side Effects, Composition And More

By Dheeraj Sharma August 26, 2024 Medicine

What Is Foeniculum Vulgare?

Foeniculum vulgare is a kind of flowering plant species that belongs to the carrot family. It is a hard, perennial herb with yellow flowers and feathery leaves. It is native to the shores of the Mediterranean but has become widely naturalized in many parts of the world. It generally grows about 2.5 meters (8 feet) tall. Fennel is widely grown, both in its local area and elsewhere, for its edible, strongly flavored leaves and fruits.

Dried fennel seeds are commonly used as a spice in minute quantities. An appropriate quantity of 100 grams of fennel seeds provides 1,440 kilojoules (345 kilocalories) of food energy. It is a rich source of protein, dietary fiber, B vitamins, and several dietary minerals, especially calcium, iron, magnesium, and manganese. Fennel seeds are 52% carbohydrates (including 40% dietary fiber), 15% fat, 16% protein, and 9% water.

Foeniculum Vulgare Chemical Constituents.

The exquisite fragrance of fennel fruits comes from volatile oils that produces a variety of scents, including trans-anethole and estragole (which smell like liquorice), fenchone (with mint and camphor notes), limonene, and 1-octen-3-ol (which has a mushroom-like scent). Foeniculum vulgare or fennel fruits also contain other phytochemicals, such as polyphenols like rosmarinic acid and luteolin, though these are present in smaller amounts.

The chemicals found in foeniculum vulgare plants, including phytochemicals and secondary compounds, could be used to create medicines like terpenoids, triterpenoid saponins, flavonoids, coumarins, polyacetylenes, and steroids. These substances have a wide variety of uses, including in food, medicine, drinks, spices, insect repellents, cosmetics, fragrances, and various industrial products.

Foeniculum vulgare Preparation

Foeniculum vulgare is commonly known as fennel, and it has a long history of use in various traditional and herbal medicine systems.

Here are some general methods of preparation of foeniculum vulgare:

  • Harvesting: Fennel is harvested throughout the growing season, but the leaves, seeds, and roots are commonly harvested at different times of season depending on the intended use of the plant. The leaves and stems are harvested fresh. The seeds are harvested when their heads are dried in fall. Whereas, the roots are harvested when the plant has finished flowering during the time of fall.
  • Drying: The harvested parts of the plant are dried for a longer duration in order to store them for a longer period. The leaves are spread, seeds, or roots in a thin layer in a well-ventilated, shaded area and allowed to fully dry for better storage and use.
  • Processing: The fresh or dried leaves are generally used whole or chopped in small pieces for teas, infusions, or as a culinary herb. The seeds are dried completely and then crushed or ground into a powder for teas, spice mixes, or other preparations. The roots of foeniculum vulgare are sliced, chopped, or grounded into a powder for tinctures, decoctions, or topical applications.

Foeniculum Vulgare Dosage

The dosage of Foeniculum vulgare varies depending on the method of consumption and the intended use. The exact dosage may depend on the individual’s health condition, age, and overall health status. 1-2 grams of dried seeds steeped in hot water to make tea can be consumed, which can be consumed 2-3 times daily. Even 0.1-0.6 mL of diluted fennel oil can be diluted and used up to three times per day. It is always recommended to first consult with a health care professional before consuming Foeniculum vulgare and confirming the right dosage, depending on the medical condition that needs to be treated through it.

Foeniculum Vulgare Dosage For Adults

The dosage of foeniculum vulgare for adults is based on which form it is administered and the medical condition that is required to be treated by it.

  • The dried seeds of Foeniculum vulgare are taken about 1-2 grams per day; they are steeped in hot water to make tea, which can be consumed later.
  • The fennel oil is first diluted for ingestion or topical application and is taken 0.1-0.6 mL per day.
  • Fennel extracts or tinctures are consumed 1-2 mL of it (1:5 ratio) too 2-3 times daily.
  • Also, 1-2 teaspoons of crushed fennel seeds are steeped in hot water for 10-15 minutes to make fennel tea.

Foeniculum Vulgare Dosage For Children

For children, the dosage of foeniculum vulgare should be lower than that for adults and customized to the child’s age and weight.

  • Foeniculum vulgare steeped in hot water to make tea can be consumed 2-3 times daily, but the amount of water and steeping time should be adjusted to suit the child’s tolerance.
  • The foeniculum vulgare oil should be diluted and used with caution in the case of a child.

It is generally not recommended for ingestion in young children unless advised by a healthcare professional. It is advised to consult a doctor before giving Foeniculum vulgare to children, especially in forms other than tea, to ensure safety and proper dosing.

Who should avoid Foeniculum Vulgare?

While foeniculum vulgare is mostly considered safe for most people when consumed in food. At the same time, there are certain individuals and situations where caution is advised when using fennel or fennel-based products. It is important to note that the severity and likelihood of these side effects and risks can vary depending on the individual, the dose, and the form of fennel used.

  • Pregnant and breastfeeding women: Foeniculum vulgare shows estrogenic effects which could possibly interfere with pregnancy or breastfeeding.
  • People with hormone-sensitive conditions: The phytoestrogens in the Foeniculum vulgare enact the effects of estrogen in the body, which leads to adverse effects for people having hormone-sensitive cancers, endometriosis, or uterine fibroids.
  • People with allergies or sensitivities: Some people may be allergic or hypersensitive to Foeniculum vulgare, which results in adverse reactions such as skin rashes, digestive distress, or respiratory problems.
  • Children: There has not been much research done on the effects of the consumption of Foeniculum vulgare in children. Hence, it is advised not to consume it. But, when it is recommended by doctors it should be only taken with proper dosage and supervision.

Potential side effects and risks of Foeniculum Vulgare:

  • Gastrointestinal issues: In some rare cases, the consumption of foeniculum vulgare may cause digestive side effects, such as heartburn, nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, or allergic reactions in some people, especially when consumed in large quantities.
  • Skin reactions: The topical application of Foeniculum vulgare oil or products containing Foeniculum vulgare may cause skin irritation, rashes, or photosensitivity in some people.
  • Hormonal disruptions: The phytoestrogen present in the Foeniculum vulgare may interfere with hormone levels in some cases, particularly in individuals with hormone-sensitive conditions.
  • Interactions with medications: Foeniculum vulgare may interact with certain medications, such as blood thinners, diabetes medications, or hormone-based therapies.
