What is Typhoid?

Typhoid, or typhoid fever, is an illness produced by the bacterium Salmonella typhi. Infectious globally through food or water, people coming from regions with poor hygiene are more likely to be affected. The bacteria infects the intestinal tract and the circulatory system and may manifest as high fever, weakness, abdominal pain, headaches, loss of appetite, and possibly rash.

If not treated early enough, the condition can complicate things such as perforation of the intestines or organ inflammations. Any deviation of the heart working properly results in the accumulation of blood in the lungs leading to early detection and treatment with antibiotics or supportive care are sometimes necessary to avoid. The best ways of handling typhoid are through regular washing, taking our vaccines, and better-purified water.

Symptoms of Typhoid Fever

  • High Fever: The one sign that is most often reported is a slowly increasing fever that can rise to 103-104 F, 40-40 C. If left untreated, it can last for several days and even weeks.
  • Weakness and Fatigue: From these symptoms it can be seen that the infection leads to tiredness and weakness such that one cannot be able to perform work.
  • Headache: Boosted headaches are normal since the body will have a compensation response to the disease and inflammation.
  • Loss of Appetite: A marked decrease in hunger results in malnutrition and appreciable weight loss.
  • Abdominal Pain: Pains or cramps in the stomach arise from inflammation in the intestines triggered by these bacteria.
  • Constipation or Diarrhea: It ranges from constipation in some cases to looser motion in others, with patients enduring different reaction rates to the treatment.
  • Rose-Colored Spots: Var, pinkish red, small round/maculopapular lesions may be seen in the chest/abdomen, characteristic in some cases.
  • Sweating: Sweating is due to a fever because the body tries to regulate its temperature in the course of the day.
  • Enlarged Liver or Spleen: In severe conditions, these organs may actually become enlarged a little and thereby may produce some feeling of pain or discomfort in the upper belly.
  • Confusion or Delirium: High severity levels of typhoid result in instances such as convulsion, dizziness, or even delirium if not treated properly.

Causes of Typhoid Fever

  • Contaminated Food and Water: Typhoid fever is primarily a food or water-borne disease caused by ingestion of food or water containing Salmonella typhi.
  • Fecal-Oral Transmission: The bacteria are disposed of with feces from an infected individual and circulate in societies where washing of hands after defecation is not observed, particularly among food handlers.
  • Poor Sanitation: Lack of a proper sewage system and contaminated water sources cause a high likelihood of contamination and typhoid.
  • Close Contact with Infected Individuals: Typhoid is communicable, so it becomes easily spread, especially if the person is a carrier of the bacteria for a long time.
  • Travel to High-Risk Areas: However, risk factors that lead to exposure exist when one is traveling to areas with a high prevalence of typhoid, including Asia, Africa, and Latin America.
  • Chronic Carriers: Even if people make a full recovery after the disease, they are capable of being carriers of the bacteria themselves — they still carry it in their gallbladder and can easily infect other people.
