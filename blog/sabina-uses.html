What is Sabina?

Fondly known as Savin or Sabina, it is a homeopathic medicine that comes from a plant called Juniperu, a small evergreen shrub usually about 1 to 4 meters in length. Plant leaves are small, dark green in colour, and scaly.

Sabina is widely used for helping with conditions involving heavy bleeding, especially during menstruation, miscarriages, or post childbirth. Sabina is also a natural medicine that has been trusted in homeopathy for a long time, and its therapeutic benefits are recognized widely for women’s health issues.

Sabina Chemical Constituents

Sabina is a multipurpose medicine used as a tranquillizer to induce abortions, as a diuretic to cause vomiting, and also to stimulate menstrual flow. Sabina’s variety of functions is due to its diverse chemical constituents.

The main components of Juniperus sabina essential oil are sabinene, terpinen-4-ol, myrtenyl acetate, and α-cadinol. The content of Sabina has several bioactive compounds, such as terpenes, lignans, flavonoids, and essential oils. The roots of Sabina have the most phenols, while the leaves contain the highest amount of lignans.

Sabina Preparation

While preparing Sabina’s medicine, utmost care was taken to ensure its effectiveness. Multiple methods were used to prepare Sabina, depending on the desired results and the required potency.

  • Plant Selection: The preparation process starts by selecting the right parts of the Juniperus sabina plant. The most commonly used parts are the fresh young twigs and leaves because they contain the highest concentration of medicinal properties.
  • Extraction: After properly cleaning the plant, the fresh leaves and twigs are crushed and soaked in alcohol. This helps draw out the essential oils, active compounds, and other beneficial substances from the plant.
  • Potentization: The mother tincture is diluted with water or alcohol in a specific ratio and shaken vigorously. This process is repeated several times to increase the potency of the medicine.

Who Should Avoid Sabina?

Sabina is a plant that has been used in traditional medicine, but it can also pose certain risks and side effects. Here are some key points regarding who should avoid it and the potential risks associated with its use:

  • Pregnant and Nursing Women: In some rare cases, Sabina can induce uterine contractions, leading to miscarriage or other complications during pregnancy. Its effects during breastfeeding are also not well-studied; hence, it is advised that consumption should be avoided.
  • Individuals with Kidney Issues: Sabina may have diuretic effects, which can strain kidneys and worsen existing kidney conditions.
  • People with Allergies: Those with known allergies to plants in the Cupressaceae family (like cypress or cedar) may also react to Sabina. Hence, it is advised not to consume it.
  • Individuals with Hormonal Disorders: Sabina may interact with hormone levels in the body, potentially affecting those with conditions like endometriosis or uterine fibroids.
  • People on Blood-Thinning Medications: In certain cases, Sabina may have anticoagulant effects, increasing the risk of bleeding when combined with blood thinners.
  • Individuals Scheduled for Surgery: Due to its potential effects on blood clotting, it is advisable to avoid Sabina at least two weeks before any surgical procedure.

What Are The Side Effects and Risks of Using Sabina?

  • Gastrointestinal Issues: Nausea, vomiting, and diarrhea could be some common side effects after using Sabina.
  • Skin Reactions: In some cases, the patient might experience a few allergic reactions that may manifest as rashes or dermatitis.
  • Kidney Damage: High doses or prolonged use of Sabina can lead to kidney irritation or damage.
  • Uterine Contractions: As mentioned, due to a lack of proper research, this can pose risks during pregnancy.
  • Electrolyte Imbalance: Due to its diuretic properties, Sabina may lead to imbalances in electrolytes.
  • Interactions with Other Medications: Potential interactions with medications for diabetes, blood pressure, and anticoagulants.
