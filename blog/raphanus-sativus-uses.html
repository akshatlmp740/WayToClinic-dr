What Is Raphanus Sativus?

Raphanus sativus is a root belonging to the Brassicaceae family of plants. It is usually found in Asia and Europe and is grown for its edible roots and leaves. The plant is characterised by long, cylindrical or spherical roots and green, leafy, edible leaves.

Apart from their culinary application, they have various medicinal and homeopathic properties such as anti-inflammatory, anti-oxidant, anti-microbial and they can act as digestive aid. The homeopathic remedy of Raphanus sativus is usually obtained from their roots and leaves which helps in the treatment of digestive and respiratory disorders.

Raphanus Sativus Chemical Constituents

Raphanus sativus, popularly known as a vegetable named radish, consists of different kinds of chemicals which are responsible for their medicinal and culinary properties. It mainly consists of isothiocyanates (sulforaphane and allyl isothiocyanate), terpenes (Limonene, alpha and beta-pinene) and sulphur compounds (dimethyl sulphide), which are responsible for antimicrobial and antioxidant properties.

Apart from that, they also contain various flavonoids (Kaempferol, Quercetin), phenolic acids (Sinapic acid, Caffeic acid), and Vitamin C, which promotes their anti-oxidant and anti-inflammatory activities. They are also rich in glucosinolates such as glucoraphanin, glucobrassicin, and gluconasturtiin, which are responsible for their taste and health benefits.

Raphanus Sativus Preparation

The preparation of Raphanus sativus is done in various ways depending on its use. For homeopathic applications, its preparation involves several steps.

  • Root selection: The step involves the selection of fresh and healthy roots of the plant Raphanus sativum.
  • Grating: After the roots are selected, they are cleaned to remove dirt particles. After cleaning is completed, the roots are grated properly.
  • Maceration: In the next step, the grated roots are placed in a container, and ethanol is added to it. The mixture is then allowed to soak for a fixed period of time (weeks) to extract the active ingredients from the root.
  • Filtration: Now, the solid part is separated from the liquid portion by filtration.
  • Potentization: The liquid portion obtained is called the tincture, which is then subjected to dilution and vigorous shaking to form substances with different potencies. Depending on the desired potency, the process is repeated many times.
