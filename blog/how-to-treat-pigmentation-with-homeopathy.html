What is Pigmentation?

Pigmentation is the coloration of the skin, hair, and eye color resulting from pigment produced in skin cells called melanocytes. The quantity and kind of melanin that is made within the skin also influence its color; eumelanin is responsible for dark brown or black color, while pheomelanin yields yellow or reddish colors.

Some influencing factors, such as exposure to the sun, hormonal fluctuations, heredity factors, and illnesses cause discoloration. Some of the known pigmentation problems are freckles, age spots, melasma, and hyperpigmentation.

Certain skin diseases, such as vitiligo, cause loss of pigmentation on the body’s surface. Some of the pigmentation problems may be prevented or minimized by using appropriate care, especially when treating skin with sunscreens and other related products.

Symptoms of Pigmentation

The symptoms can vary depending on the underlying cause, but common signs include

  • Uneven Skin Tone: This is a common skin problem that happens when some parts of the body become brighter or darker than the rest of the skin. It can be due to sun damage, hormonal fluctuations, or skin diseases such as acne scarring.
  • Freckles: Small, rounded, tan, or brown markings located on the skin of the face, shoulders, or arm that result from the skin’s reaction to sun exposure. They are hereditary and frequent in individuals with fair skin.
  • Age Spots: These are common skin changes, also referred to as liver spots; they are brown, flat lesions that develop due to exposure to the sun over time. These normally manifest themselves on parts such as the face, hands and arms as people grow old.
  • Melasma: The most common morphological appearances are brown or gray-brown macules that favor the face in women. It is believed to be due to fluctuating hormone levels that happen during pregnancy, the use of birth control pills, or hormone replacement therapies.
  • Sunspots: These are larger, dark spots that tend to be a result of extended exposure to the sun. They are often seen cropping on the parts that are normally bare naked to the sun, for instance, the face, neck, and hand.
  • Vitiligo: A disease characterized by the absence of color on the skin, resulting in the formation of patches on the skin. It is not well understood what causes it, but it is believed to be an autoimmune disease.
  • Hyperpigmentation: Compensatory hyperpigmentation can be defined as the phenomenon of increasing skin pigmentation in some areas; it may be the result of inflammation, injury, or skin disease, such as acne. It originates from an excessive synthesis of melanin in those areas of the body.
  • Hypopigmentation: Skin whitening, which is the depletion of skin color and the formation of light-colored areas. This may be caused by some factors such as vitiligo, scarring, or skin conditions that hinder melanocytes from proper secretion of the pigment.

Causes of Pigmentation

Here are the main causes:

  • Sun Exposure: UV radiation increases melanin synthesis, which results in tanning, sunburns, or hyperpigmentation.
  • Hormonal Changes: The hormonal changes that are associated with pregnancy or menopause, for example, can cause melasma.
  • Inflammation or Injury: PIH can be produced after skin damage such as injury, acne, or even simple burns.
  • Genetic Factors: The cause of pigment disorders may be hereditary, but the onset is usually later in life.
  • Certain Medications: Some of the drugs may lead to pigmentation as a side effect say chemotherapy agents or oral contraceptives.
  • Aging: Unfortunately, over time, melanin production is no longer uniform, and skin becomes patchy and speckled.
  • Underlying Health Issues: Other diseases, such as Addison’s disease or liver disorders, make changes in melanin manufacture and lead to pigmentation.
  • Chemical Exposure: Sulfates found in skincare products are claimed to cause skin irritation and, hence pigmentation.
