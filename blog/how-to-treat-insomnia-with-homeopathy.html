What is Insomnia?

Insomnia is when you’re not getting the sleep you need. It might mean not getting enough sleep, having difficulty sleeping, or waking abruptly at night. Some find it a minor bother, while others find it a big problem. The reasons for insomnia vary.

Your body requires sleep for many reasons, although science is still uncovering why it’s so crucial. Lack of sleep leads to sleep deprivation, which is generally unpleasant and hampers your ability to function at your best. Understanding and addressing the causes of insomnia are essential for better sleep and overall well-being.

Types of Insomnia

Experts categorize insomnia in two ways to help understand it better;

Firstly, based on time, there are acute insomnia (short-term) and chronic insomnia (long-term). The prolonged form is officially termed insomnia disorder.

Secondly, considering the cause, there’s primary insomnia, which occurs independently, and secondary insomnia, a symptom of another condition or circumstance.

So, whether it’s a temporary or more persistent issue, and whether it stands alone or is connected to something else, these classifications aid in addressing and treating different types of insomnia.

Symptoms of Insomnia

Insomnia comes with various symptoms, such as;

Trouble Sleeping

It may involve insufficient sleep, difficulty initiating sleep, or waking up in the middle of the night. These variations in sleep initiation and maintenance are key indicators of individuals’ difficulties in achieving a restful night.

Initial (sleep onset) insomnia: Difficulty falling asleep

Middle (maintenance) insomnia: This impacts around two-thirds of individuals experiencing insomnia who awaken during the night but manage to return to sleep

Late (early waking) insomnia: Rising too early in the morning and struggling to resume sleep

Daytime Effects

The impact of insomnia extends beyond the night, affecting one’s wakeful hours. The daytime effects shed light on the pervasive consequences of inadequate sleep, such as;

  • Feeling tired, unwell, or sleepy
  • Delayed responses, like slow reactions while driving
  • Difficulty remembering things
  • Difficulty concentrating slowed thinking, or confusion
  • Mood disturbances, notably anxiety, depression, and irritability
  • Disruptions in work, social activities, hobbies, or routine activities

Chronic Insomnia Characteristics

When the struggle to sleep persists over the long term, it enters the realm of chronic insomnia. Specific characteristics include;

  • Circumstances: Chronic insomnia is diagnosed when sleep difficulties persist despite having the right time and environment for sleep, without external factors like changes in work schedule interfering
  • Frequency: Chronic insomnia happens regularly, happening a minimum of three times per week
  • Duration: It extends for at least three months
  • Explanation: Chronic insomnia is diagnosed when sleep troubles aren’t due to substances, medications, other sleep disorders, or underlying medical or mental health conditions

Causes of Insomnia

While experts haven’t unraveled all the mysteries surrounding insomnia, various factors contribute to or potentially cause this condition;

  • Family History (Genetics): Insomnia can be hereditary, with sleep traits and conditions often passing down through families. The role of genetics in shaping our susceptibility to insomnia sheds light on the importance of familial history in understanding and addressing sleep difficulties.
  • Brain Activity Differences: Individuals with insomnia may exhibit differences in brain activity or chemistry that impact their ability to achieve restful sleep. This variation in neural function underscores the intricate connection between our brain processes and sleep quality.
  • Medical Conditions: The state of your physical health significantly influences your capacity to sleep. Temporary ailments like infections or injuries, along with chronic conditions such as acid reflux or Parkinson’s disease, can disrupt sleep patterns. Factors impacting your body’s internal sleep/wake cycle, known as circadian rhythm, contribute to this.
  • Mental Health Conditions: Mental well-being: About half of individuals experiencing chronic insomnia also grapple with other mental health issues like anxiety or depression. Recognizing the interplay between mental well-being and sleep underscores the holistic approach needed for effective insomnia management.
  • Life Circumstances: While stressful events may not directly cause insomnia, they frequently contribute to its development. Understanding how life stressors affect sleep enables a more thorough approach to tackling the underlying causes of insomnia.
  • Life Changes: Both temporary adjustments, such as jet lag or sleeping in an unfamiliar location, and long-term changes, such as moving to a new house, can affect sleep habits. Acknowledging the impact of life transitions on sleep emphasizes the need for adaptive strategies to maintain healthy sleep routines.
  • Habits and Routine: How you manage your sleep, termed sleep hygiene, plays a crucial role in the development of insomnia. Factors like napping, bedtime routines, caffeine consumption, and other habits influence sleep quality, emphasizing the importance of cultivating positive sleep practices for overall well-being.

Diet And Lifestyle Changes To Deal With Insomnia

Dealing with insomnia requires more than just counting sheep. Minor modifications to your everyday schedule, especially in terms of diet and lifestyle, can significantly influence your capacity to enjoy a restful night’s sleep. Let’s delve into these uncomplicated yet impactful diet and lifestyle adjustments to enhance the quality of your sleep.

Diet for Insomnia Patients

Incorporating certain foods into your evening routine can help fight insomnia and may drift you into sound sleep;

  • Chamomile Tea: Swap your regular tea for chamomile tea before bedtime. Chamomile boosts the immune system and contains the antioxidant Apigenin, which binds receptors in the brain, inducing sleepiness and preventing insomnia.
  • Watermelons: Eating a cup of diced watermelon pieces before bedtime keeps you hydrated and curbs your hunger after dinner. However, they consume in moderation due to their high water content, which might lead to midnight trips to the washroom.
  • Pistachios: Rich in essential nutrients, including vitamin B6 and magnesium, pistachios can aid sleep. Consume them moderately, as excessive calorie intake can hinder sleep.
  • Walnuts: Walnuts regulate the sleep hormone Melatonin, improving overall sleep quality. They also provide ALA, an omega-3 fatty acid that stimulates the generation of serotonin, a brain chemical that enhances sleep.
  • White Rice: High in carbohydrates and low in fiber, white rice with a high glycemic index may enhance sleep quality when consumed a few hours before bedtime.
  • Bananas: A rich source of magnesium and tryptophan, bananas help induce a good night’s sleep, acting as a muscle and nerve relaxant.
  • Prunes: Like walnuts, prunes promote Melatonin production and increase calcium, magnesium, and vitamin B6 levels.
  • Dark Chocolate: Dark chocolate raises Serotonin levels, promoting relaxation of the mind and body.
  • Kiwis: Nutrient-rich kiwis are suggested as one of the best foods to consume before bedtime, aiding quick sleep onset and ensuring uninterrupted sleep.
  • Almonds: Packed with healthy nutrients, almonds are rich in magnesium, known to benefit individuals with insomnia. They also help regulate the stress hormone Cortisol, preventing sleep disruptions.
  • Turkey: Turkey, with its tryptophan content, increases melatonin production, potentially explaining post-meal tiredness. Consuming moderate protein amounts before bed is associated with improved sleep quality.
  • Fatty Fish: Fatty fish, such as salmon, mackerel, trout, and tuna, rich in omega-3 fatty acids and vitamin D, can enhance sleep quality by increasing serotonin production.

Lifestyle Changes to Copup With Insomnia

In addition to dietary adjustments, incorporating specific lifestyle changes can significantly impact your sleep;

  • Avoid caffeine after lunch
  • Eat light at dinner also at least two hours before bedtime
  • Limit liquid intake before bed
  • Maintain a dark, comfortable bedroom temperature
  • Limit screen time before sleep
  • Exercise daily
  • Avoid stimulating activities before bed
  • Consider incorporating yoga and meditation into your routine
  • Establish a regular bedtime routine
  • Keep a sleep diary to monitor sleep patterns and identify potential factors affecting sleep
