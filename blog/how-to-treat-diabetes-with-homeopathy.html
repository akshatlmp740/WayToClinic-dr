How To Treat Diabetes With Homeopathy?

By Deeksha Katiyar January 22, 2024 Medical Conditions

Summary: Homeopathy can complement conventional diabetes management strategies and can contribute to better blood sugar control when used in harmony with lifestyle adjustments and mainstream treatments. While homeopathic treatment for diabetes may offer potential benefits in diabetes management, it does not claim to be a cure for the condition.

Diabetes is a health condition in which your blood sugar (glucose) levels become too high. This occurs when your pancreas does not produce enough insulin, or your body does not utilize it adequately. It can affect anyone, and most types are long-term but manageable with medications or lifestyle changes.

Your body gets glucose mainly from the carbs in your food. It’s the energy source your body uses, carried to cells by your blood. Think of insulin as the key to helping glucose reach its destination. If your pancreas does not produce enough insulin or your body does not use it properly, glucose builds up, resulting in high blood sugar.

Consistently high blood glucose over time can lead to issues like heart disease, nerve damage, and eye problems. The technical term for this condition is diabetes mellitus, which is different from diabetes insipidus, which is rarer but shares the name due to increased thirst and frequent urination. Managing diabetes involves medications, lifestyle adjustments, and understanding how glucose and insulin function in your body.

Types of Diabetes

Understanding diabetes involves knowing the different types it comes in. Here are the various forms of diabetes;

  • Type 2 Diabetes: The most common type, where your body doesn’t make not produce enough insulin and cells do not respond well to it. It is mostly seen in adults, but children can have it, too.
  • Prediabetes: This stage precedes Type 2 diabetes. Blood glucose levels are above normal but not high enough to be diagnosed with Type 2 diabetes.
  • Type 1 Diabetes: Type 1 Diabetes is an autoimmune illness in which the immune system assaults insulin-producing cells. Typically diagnosed in children and young adults, however, it may happen at any age.
  • Gestational Diabetes: Gestational diabetes is a condition that develops during pregnancy and usually goes away. However, it puts you at a higher risk of Type 2 diabetes later in life.

Other Types of Diabetes

Now, here are some less familiar but equally noteworthy forms of diabetes. These variations, though rarer, give information about the diverse nature of this condition. Understanding these broadens our understanding of diabetes and its intricacies;

  • Type 3c Diabetes: Occurs when your pancreas is damaged, affecting insulin production. It is linked to conditions like pancreatitis, pancreatic cancer, cystic fibrosis, and hemochromatosis.
  • Latent Autoimmune Diabetes in Adults (LADA): It is similar to Type 1 but develops more slowly, usually in people over 30.
  • Maturity-Onset Diabetes of the Young (MODY): Caused by an inherited genetic mutation affecting insulin production. Approximately 5% of people with diabetes are affected by it.
  • Neonatal Diabetes: A rare form occurring in the first six months of life. It can be permanent or transient, disappearing within a few months but potentially returning later.
  • Brittle Diabetes: A severe form of Type 1 diabetes marked by frequent and severe blood sugar level episodes, sometimes requiring a pancreas transplant.

Symptoms of Diabetes

Recognizing the symptoms of diabetes is critical for proper treatment. Here are the symptoms that may indicate diabetes, keeping it straightforward and understandable for those new to this medical information. Some common symptoms include;

  • Increased Thirst (Polydipsia) and Dry Mouth: Feeling excessively thirsty and having a dry mouth can be early signs of diabetes. Paying attention to these cues is essential.
  • Frequent Urination: If you find yourself making more trips to the bathroom than usual, it could be a symptom of diabetes. Your body may be trying to eliminate excess sugar through urine.
  • Fatigue: Persistent tiredness or fatigue can be a signal that your body is not effectively using glucose for energy, a common occurrence in diabetes.
  • Blurred Vision: Diabetes can affect your eyesight, leading to blurred vision. Diabetes patients require regular eye examinations.
  • Unexplained Weight Loss: Unusual weight loss without diet or activity modifications may indicate diabetes. This occurs when the body starts using muscle and fat for energy.
  • Numbness or Tingling in Hands or Feet: Diabetes can cause nerve damage, resulting in numbness or tingling in the hands and feet.
  • Slow-Healing Sores or Cuts: Wounds that take longer to heal than normal may suggest diabetes. High blood sugar levels might disrupt the body’s natural healing process.
  • Frequent Skin and Vaginal Yeast Infections: Elevated sugar levels create an environment conducive to yeast growth, leading to recurrent infections. This is especially relevant for those with diabetes.

Additional Symptoms Based on Diabetes Type

Understanding diabetes involves recognizing specific symptoms linked to its various types. Below are some additional symptoms associated with each type, shedding light on how diabetes can manifest differently;

  • Type 1 Diabetes: Symptoms may develop quickly, and severe complications may arise, such as diabetes-related ketoacidosis (DKA), characterized by vomiting, stomach pains, fruity-smelling breath, and labored breathing.
  • Type 2 Diabetes and Prediabetes: Symptoms may appear gradually, and you may not notice them. Routine blood testing might detect elevated blood sugar levels. Darkened skin in certain areas may be a sign of prediabetes.
  • Gestational Diabetes: Typically, gestational diabetes may not show noticeable symptoms. Testing between 24 and 28 weeks of pregnancy helps in early detection.

Causes of Diabetes

Diabetes is a health condition where there’s too much sugar in your blood. This happens when your body can’t handle it properly. There are different causes behind high blood sugar levels. Below, we have explained how diabetes develops;

  • Insulin Resistance: Type 2 diabetes comes from a problem called insulin resistance. This indicates your body does not respond appropriately to insulin, a vital factor in sugar regulation. Causes include being higher weight, not moving enough, your diet, hormones, genetics, and some medications.
  • Autoimmune Disease: Type 1 diabetes and LADA are caused by your immune system erroneously attacking insulin-producing pancreatic cells. It’s like the body turning against itself.
  • Hormonal Imbalances: Sometimes, hormonal changes can lead to diabetes. During pregnancy, certain hormones create resistance to insulin, causing gestational diabetes. Conditions like acromegaly and Cushing syndrome can also trigger Type 2 diabetes.
  • Pancreatic Damage: Your pancreas, an organ crucial for insulin production, may face issues due to damage from various sources. Conditions, surgeries, or injuries affecting the pancreas can lead to Type 3c diabetes.
  • Genetic Mutations: Some diabetes types result from genetic factors. Specific genetic mutations cause MODY and neonatal diabetes. It’s like a genetic instruction that controls how your body processes sugar.
  • Medication Effects: Certain medications, especially those for HIV/AIDS or corticosteroids, can also bring about Type 2 diabetes with prolonged use. It is critical to be informed of the possible adverse effects of drugs.

Diet and Lifestyle changes to deal with Diabetes

Dietary choices and lifestyle adjustments are important when managing diabetes. Understanding the impact of food, maintaining a healthy weight, and incorporating physical activity are crucial components. Here are the best practical diet and lifestyle changes to effectively deal with diabetes;

  • Managing Weight: Maintaining a healthy weight is pivotal for Type 2 diabetes management, reducing the risk of complications. Strategies include adopting a balanced diet and engaging in regular physical activity. Weight reduction is accomplished by consuming fewer calories than expended through daily activities.
  • Eating Healthy: Choosing nutritious foods, controlling portion sizes, and reading food labels are vital for weight management and diabetes prevention. For those with prediabetes or diabetes, limiting simple carbohydrates found in sugary items is crucial to regulating blood glucose levels. One must include the following in their diet;
    • Fiber-rich
    • Whole grains
    • Non-fried fish high in omega-3 fatty acids
    • Lean meats
    • Chicken or turkey (skinless)
    • Fruits
    • Vegetables
    • Nuts
    • Seeds
    • Legumes
  • Limit: The body responds to different meals, and some dietary items have the potential to raise your blood sugar level to concern levels. Keeping a food and blood glucose log helps track the impact of foods on blood sugar levels. The foods that we should cut or limit in our diet include;
    • Sweets
    • Added sugars
    • Fatty and processed meats
    • Sodium
    • Cholesterol-rich foods
    • Saturated fats
    • Trans fats
    • Alcohol
  • Healthy Eating and Busy Lifestyle: Despite busy schedules, prioritizing healthy eating is achievable. Tips include bringing a nutritious lunch and snacks, reducing caffeine intake, staying hydrated, and preparing healthy meals for the family.
  • Regular Physical Activity: Doing a minimum of 30 minutes of moderate-intensity aerobic exercise most days of the week benefits general health and significantly lowers the risk of diabetes. For those already diagnosed, physical activity aids in disease control and minimizing health risks.

Healthy people should engage in at least 150 minutes of moderate-intensity aerobic activity or 75 minutes of vigorous-intensity activity each week. This can be accomplished by activities such as brisk walking, jogging, or a combination of the two.
