Hypericum Perforatum – Uses, Dosage, Side Effects, Composition And More

What Is Hypericum Perforatum?

Hypericum perforatum is commonly known as St John’s wort. It is a type of flowering plant in the family of Hypericaceae. Hypericum perforatum is a perennial plant that grows up to one meter tall, with multiple yellow flowers that have clearly visible black glands around their edges, long stamens (male reproductive organs), and three pistils (female reproductive organs).

Hypericum perforatum plant is local to temperate regions across Eurasia and North Africa and has been introduced to East Asia, Australia, New Zealand, and parts of North and South America. The root of the hypericum perforatum plant is slender and woody, featuring numerous small, fibrous side roots as well as extensive, creeping rhizomes. The central root of the plant can extend to a depth of 0.6–1.5 meters into the soil, depending on the growing conditions and environment. Also, its root crown is woody.

Hypericum Perforatum Chemical Constituents

Hypericum perforatum contains multiple types of bioactive compounds, including naphthodianthrones like hypericin and pseudohypericin, flavonoids such as hyperoside, isoquercetin, and rutin, and phloroglucinols like adhyperforin and hyperforin. It is used in particularly treating effectively mild to moderate depression.

The therapeutic effects of hypericum perforatum are attributed to a diverse range of compounds, including naphthodianthrones (hypericin), phloroglucinol derivatives (hyperforin), and various phenolics (e.g., gallic acid, protocatechuic acid, chlorogenic acid, rosmarinic acid), as well as flavonoids like hyperoside, isoquercetin, quercitrin, quercetin, and rutin.

Who Should Avoid Hypericum Perforatum?

Hypericum perforatum is a widely used herbal medicine, but it is not suitable for everyone. Here are a few groups of people who should avoid using hypericum perforatum, as well as the potential side effects and risks associated with its use:

  • Individuals Taking Prescription Medications: The interactions with various kinds of drugs such as antidepressants, anticoagulants (blood thinners), anticonvulsants, immunosuppressants, oral contraceptives, and chemotherapy drugs can reduce the effectiveness of the medications or increase the risk of side effects.
  • Pregnant and Breastfeeding Women: There is a potential risk of harm to the developing fetus or nursing infant in the women; hence is advised for pregnant and breastfeeding women to consume it.
  • Children and Adolescents: Due to a lack of reliable data on the appropriate dosing and potential risks for this age group, it is recommended not to intake it without consulting a doctor.
  • Individuals with Certain Medical Conditions: People with certain medical conditions like bipolar disorder or a history of mania, diabetes, glaucoma, neurological disorders (e.g., seizures), and liver or kidney disease should avoid its use.

Potential Side Effects and Risks of Hypericum Perforatum:

  • Gastrointestinal effects such as nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain, and diarrhea.
  • Neurological effects like dizziness, headache, fatigue, and confusion.
  • Skin and photosensitivity like that of increased sun sensitivity, rashes, and skin irritation.
  • Cardiovascular effects like elevated heart rate, and irregular heartbeat.
  • Hypericum perforatum can significantly interact with a wide range of prescription drugs, resulting in reducing their effectiveness or increasing the risk of side effects.
  • Hypericum perforatum also has some rare cases of triggering manic episodes in individuals with bipolar disorder.
  • When combined with certain antidepressants, hypericum perforatum increases the risk of serotonin syndrome, a potentially life-threatening condition.
