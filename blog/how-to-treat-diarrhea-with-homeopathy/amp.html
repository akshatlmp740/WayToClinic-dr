What is Diarrhea?

Diarrhea is commonly described as passing more than three loose bowel movements in a single day. It can be categorized based on its duration; it may be acute, which lasts for 1-2 days; persistent, 2-4 weeks; and chronic, which lasts for more than 4 weeks.

Some of the more familiar ones are caused by viruses (rotavirus), bacteria (Escherichia coli, Salmonella), or parasites and are usually food or water-borne. Other causes include food intolerance, certain medications, or underlying health complications of IBS or IBD.

Symptoms of Diarrhea

Diarrhea is the passing of loose and watery stools and is commonly experienced three or more times within a day. In addition to this primary symptom, individuals may experience a range of other symptoms, including:

  • Urgent Need to Use the Bathroom: The feeling of an urgent, or pressing, need to defecate.
  • Abdominal Cramping: Discomfort in the stomach area because of some augmented movement from within the gut.
  • Nausea: Nausea may result in vomiting, which is stomach discomfort that may be described as a knot.
  • Pain in the Abdomen: Less Specific as any feeling of uneasiness or severe paroxysmal pain in the abdominal area.
  • Bloating: Inflammation or enlargement of the abdomen as the result of accumulation of gasses or fluids.
  • Loss of Control of Bowel Movements: Failure to inhibit actions, which results in mishaps.
  • Fever and Chills: Symptoms of another ailment might show their symptoms in a patient’s progress.
  • Bloody Stools: It’s bloody diarrhea, which means that it may be much worse than just a mere stomach bug.
  • Light-headedness and Dizziness: Usually associated with the loss of body fluids in some way or another.
  • Vomiting: This may include diarrhea, especially in cases of infection.

Causes of Diarrhea

Diarrhea can happen for many reasons, including infections and health problems. Here are the main causes:

  • Infections:
    • Viruses: These include regular ones such as the norovirus and rotavirus, whose effects are normally short-term, leading to diarrhea.
    • Bacteria: Consuming food and water that contain these pathogens can lead to foodborne pathogens such as bacteria from Salmonella, E. coli, and Campylobacter.
    • Parasites: Other water-borne parasites include Giardia and Cryptosporidium, which lead to diarrhea.
  • Food-Related Causes:
    • Food Intolerances: Issues such as lactose intolerance are known to result in diarrhea after the consumption of specific foods.
    • Food Allergies: Other food sensitivities, including gluten in celiac disease, can lead to diarrhea and or other digestive issues.
  • Medications:
    • Antibiotics: These can disrupt the normal healthy bacteria found in the stomach and thus cause diarrhea.
    • Other Medicines: Diarrhea may also be brought about by laxatives and some of the treatments used for cancer.
  • Digestive Disorders:
    • Irritable Bowel Syndrome (IBS): This results in cases of diarrhea and constipation.
    • Inflammatory Bowel Disease (IBD): Crohn’s disease or ulcerative colitis is one of the prevalent chronic diseases that cause diarrhea coupled with abdominal pain all the time.
  • Surgery: Digestion-related surgeries may alter the way food is channeled in the intestines, leading to diarrhea.
  • Other Conditions:
    • Metabolic Issues: Conditions such as hyperthyroidism, which hastens digestion and leads to diarrhea, may cause prolonged healing.
    • Traveler’s Diarrhea: It is caused by the consumption of contaminated food and/or water while in other countries, especially those that are developing.
    • Stress: Pressure and stress within the bowels of the stomach have the effect of indigestion and diarrhea.
    • Chronic Infections: Chronic infections caused by bacteria or parasites.
    • Cancer: Diarrhea is a symptom of some cancers in the digestive system.
