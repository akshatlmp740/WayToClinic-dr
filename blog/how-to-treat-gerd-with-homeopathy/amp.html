What is GERD?

Gastroesophageal Reflux Disease (GERD) is a chronic digestive disorder affecting the lower esophageal sphincter, a muscular ring between the stomach and esophagus. When this muscle fails to close properly after food has entered the stomach, acid from the stomach can flow back into the esophagus, causing irritation and inflammation.

Heartburn, regurgitation, difficulty swallowing, and chest pain are all symptoms of GERD. Some people may also experience hoarseness or persistent coughing.

While occasional acid reflux is normal for most people, those who experience symptoms more than twice per week should consult with a healthcare provider to determine if they have GERD. This condition can cause complications such as ulcers in the esophagus or even increase ones risk for cancer.

Fortunately, numerous treatment options are available, including conventional medicine and natural remedies like homeopathy.

Symptoms Of GERD

Millions of people around the world suffer from gastroesophageal reflux disease (GERD). One of the primary ways to identify whether you have GERD is by looking out for its symptoms. These symptoms are:

  • Chest pain: Chest pain is one of the most common symptoms of GERD. This pain is caused by the stomach acid flowing back into your esophagus and irritating the lining.
  • Nausea: A feeling of unease and discomfort in the stomach, often leading to vomiting, is one of the most common symptoms of GERD.
  • Pain while swallowing: Pain while swallowing is one of the most common symptoms of GERD. It can occur in any part of your throat, including the back of your mouth, tonsils, or esophagus. This condition is known as odynophagia and can be quite distressing for many people. Some people may also experience pain when they are eating solid foods, while others may only have trouble with soft foods.
  • Heartburn: Heartburn is a common symptom of GERD and can be quite uncomfortable. Irritation occurs when stomach acid backs up into the esophagus.
  • Chronic cough or hoarseness: Chronic cough or hoarseness is a common symptom of GERD. This type of cough can last for weeks or even months, making it challenging to identify its root cause. Many people mistake it for allergies or asthma, but these conditions are unlikely to cause chronic coughing without other symptoms, such as wheezing or shortness of breath.
  • Loss of appetite: Another GERD symptom that shouldnt be ignored is loss of appetite. Acid reflux can trigger this loss of appetite, among other things.
  • Upper abdominal pain: Upper abdominal pain is a common symptom of GERD that people of all ages can experience. The pain may also spread to other areas, such as your chest or back, making it difficult to pinpoint the exact location. One thing to keep in mind is that upper abdominal pain caused by GERD typically occurs after eating, especially if youve had a large meal.
  • The frequent sour taste of acid: One of the most uncomfortable symptoms of GERD is experiencing a frequent sour taste of acid in your mouth. The sour taste can be quite unpleasant and may worsen after eating certain foods or lying down.
  • The feeling of choking: One of the symptoms of GERD that can be especially frightening is the feeling of choking. This sensation may make you feel like something is stuck in your throat or having trouble breathing.
  • Bad breath: Halitosis occurs when stomach acid backs up into the esophagus. This can be embarrassing for individuals who suffer from it, especially if they are not aware that GERD causes it.

If youre experiencing any of these symptoms regularly, it might be a good idea to consult a doctor and get an accurate diagnosis before starting any treatment plan.

Causes Of GERD

Acid from the stomach flows back into the esophagus, causing gastroesophageal reflux disease (GERD). In addition to occasional heartburn, chronic GERD can lead to more serious health issues if left untreated. Understanding the causes of GERD is crucial in preventing its occurrence.

  • Smoking and tobacco: Smoking and tobacco use is a major risk factor for GERD. As a result, smokers are more likely to experience reflux symptoms such as heartburn and regurgitation.
  • Obesity: GERD is commonly caused by obesity. When a person is overweight, it puts pressure on their stomach, which can cause their stomach acid to travel back up into the esophagus.
  • Pregnancy: Pregnancy also increases the risk of GERD or acid reflux disease. During pregnancy, progesterone relaxes the muscles in the lower esophageal sphincter (LES), allowing stomach acid to flow back into the esophagus.
  • Incompetent Lower Esophageal Sphincter (LES): Between the stomach and esophagus, the LES acts as a barrier. When the LES relaxes at inappropriate times or doesnt close properly, it allows acid to flow up into the esophagus.
  • Certain Foods and Beverages: Spicy foods, chocolate, and tomato-based products are just some examples that may trigger an episode by relaxing or irritating gastric contents, allowing them to enter back into our food pipe.
  • Hiatal Hernia: A hiatus hernia can cause acid reflux and heartburn, both GERD symptoms.
  • Alcohol: Alcohol is a common cause of GERD or gastroesophageal reflux disease. This can lead to heartburn and discomfort.
  • Medication: Medication is one of the causes of GERD. Certain medications can weaken or relax the lower esophageal sphincter (LES), a muscle that prevents stomach acid from flowing back into the esophagus. Heartburn and other GERD symptoms can result from this. Some common medications that may cause GERD include:
    • Ibuprofen, naproxen, and aspirin are nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs)
    • Calcium channel blockers are used to treat high blood pressure
    • Antidepressants include tricyclics and selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors (SSRIs)
    • Sedatives and tranquilizers used for anxiety or insomnia
    • Beta-blockers prescribed for heart disease

Lifestyle Changes for GERD Patients

In order to manage the symptoms of GERD, lifestyle changes are crucial. One of the simplest yet effective ways to avoid heartburn is by changing your diet and eating habits.

  • Avoid foods:
    • Avoid trigger foods such as spicy, acidic, or fatty foods, chocolate, alcohol, and caffeine.
    • Eat smaller meals more frequently instead of one large meal to help reduce pressure on the LES (lower esophageal sphincter).
    • Chew food slowly and thoroughly before swallowing.
  • Maintaining a healthy weight: It also helps manage GERD symptoms since excess weight can put pressure on your stomach, which increases the likelihood of acid reflux. Regular exercise can aid with weight loss while reducing stress levels that may exacerbate GERD symptoms.
  • Avoid lying down immediately after eating: If you want to reduce the risk of acid reflux after eating, wait at least 2-3 hours before lying down or going to bed. Elevate your head while sleeping using pillows or an adjustable bed frame.
  • Quit smoking: Smoking weakens the LES, which allows stomach acids to flow back into the esophagus, causing heartburn and other GERD symptoms.

By incorporating these lifestyle changes into a daily routine along with homeopathic remedies for GERD treatment, patients can better manage their condition so they can live life symptom-free.
