What is a Frozen Shoulder?

Frozen shoulder is a painful disorder that affects the shoulder joint and is commonly referred to as adhesive capsulitis. It affects the shoulder because the connective tissues around the area thicken and become sore, making it difficult for flexibility. The condition often comes slowly. Sufferers will primarily feel pain, which gets worse with time, before feeling stiff.

Frozen shoulder usually progresses through three stages: the freezing stage, during which pain rises and movement numbers drop; the frozen stage, where pain may decrease but stiffness remains; and the thawing stage, in which movement starts to get better. It is prevalent in humans between the ages of forty and sixty years.

Still, it can be afflicting to anyone, especially those who have been idle for a long time due to injuries or operations. Such treatment involves physical therapy, pain relievers, and sometimes corticosteroid injections. Recovery may take between a few months to a few years, but many patients are likely to achieve substantial improvement concerning shoulder function.

Symptoms of Frozen Shoulder

Frozen shoulder, or adhesive capsulitis, develops gradually and is marked by shoulder pain and stiffness, progressing through three phases:

  • Freezing Stage
    • Pain: Discharge, which may be described as thick, chunky, bony-hard or hard, white, curdy, and cheesy; Persistent dull or aching pain that increases with movement and is worse at night.
    • Limited Movement: Reduced mobility that affects ordinary functioning.
  • Frozen Stage
    • Reduced Pain: Pain also decreases, but patients experience significant restrictedness of motion.
    • Restricted Motion: Severe restriction of active shoulder range, involving the inability to raise an arm overhead or backward.
  • Thawing Stage
    • Improved Mobility: Shoulder movement regains gradually despite a slight loss of movement range.
    • Residual Pain: In other words, movement is directly proportional to the reduction of pain.

Causes of Frozen Shoulder

Frozen shoulder, or adhesive capsulitis, occurs when the connective tissue surrounding the shoulder joint thickens and tightens, causing pain and restricted movement. While its exact cause is unclear, several factors may contribute:

  • Immobilization: Sedentary lifestyles, such as following an accident or surgery or paralysis from a stroke.
  • Age and Gender: Typically occurring in middle-aged non-Hispanic women between the ages of 40 and 60.
  • Health Conditions:
    • Diabetes: Affects 10%-20% of people with diabetes.
    • Thyroid Disorders: Hypothyroidism And Hyperthyroidism.
    • Cardiovascular Disease: Linked to higher risk.
    • Parkinson’s Disease: Increases incidence.
  • Shoulder Injuries: Prior conditioning issues such as prior shoulder injuries such as rotator cuff tears or bursitis.
  • Inflammatory Conditions: Inflammation that arises from overuse or repeated strain.
  • Genetics: Existing evidence in the family as a positive review of the condition.
  • Other Factors: Cancer, poor sleep quality, and constipation likelihood.
