What Is Hyperpigmentation?

Hyperpigmentation is a common skin condition in which specific areas become darker than the rest due to excess melanin production. These darkened spots can appear in various colors, such as brown, black, gray, red, or pink, and are often referred to as age spots, sun spots, or liver spots. They can appear in one specific area or be spread across different parts of the body.

Symptoms Of Hyperpigmentation

Symptoms of hyperpigmentation typically manifest as darkened patches on the skin without any accompanying discomfort or other noticeable effects. These patches can vary in size and appear anywhere on the body. If you notice skin spots along with additional symptoms, it’s advisable to consult your primary care provider or a dermatologist for further evaluation and appropriate management.

What Causes Hyperpigmentation?

The color of our skin is determined by melanin, a pigment produced by skin cells. When these cells are damaged or unhealthy, they may overproduce melanin, causing darkened areas to form. Several factors can contribute to hyperpigmentation;

  • Conditions like Addison’s disease, where the body fails to produce enough cortisol, can lead to skin discoloration
  • Individuals with a family history of freckles or other pigmentation issues may be more prone to developing hyperpigmentation.
  • Fluctuations in hormones during puberty, pregnancy, or due to medical conditions can trigger increased melanin production.
  • Skin problems such as acne, cuts, or burns can result in postinflammatory hyperpigmentation, where affected areas darken as they heal.
  • Certain drugs, including oral contraceptives and medications that heighten sensitivity to light, can contribute to skin pigmentation changes.
  • Melasma is a specific type of hyperpigmentation often triggered by hormonal changes and sun exposure.
  • Inadequate intake of vitamins like B12 and folic acid may impact skin health and contribute to pigmentation irregularities.
  • Overexposure to sunlight can lead to the development of solar lentigines, commonly known as sun spots.
  • Thyroid Disorders

Lifestyle And Skin Care To Deal With Hyperpigmentation

Maintaining a healthy lifestyle and incorporating specific skin care practices can significantly help in managing hyperpigmentation;

  • Protect your skin from the sun by using a broad-spectrum sunscreen with at least SPF 30 every day. Sun exposure increases melanin production, leading to uneven pigmentation.
  • Avoid harsh scrubs and use gentle exfoliants like AHAs and BHAs. Incorporate products with niacinamide, vitamin C, and retinol to inhibit melanin production and improve skin tone.
